In 2040, Your Grocery Receipt Now Shows an “EI Score.” For Some Families, It’s a Source of Pride — and Guilt.

A quiet number at checkout has become a daily ritual — and a new fault line between households that can afford low-intensity living and those that cannot.

Maria’s score 42 ↓ from 51 last month

When Maria Lopez, a 38-year-old nurse in Phoenix, scans her groceries at the self-checkout, a small number appears at the bottom of her digital receipt: EI Intensity: 42. It is lower than last month’s 51. She smiles.

Across the United States and dozens of other countries, major retailers now display a standardized Environmental Impact Intensity score — a simplified version of the corporate EI/Revenue metrics companies have been required to publish since the mid-2030s. The number reflects the estimated environmental footprint (carbon, water stress, and land use) embedded in each product, normalized for price.

For many households, the score has become a quiet daily ritual. Parents compare cereal brands. College students track their monthly “EI budget” the way earlier generations watched calories. Community groups in several states have created neighborhood leaderboards, turning lower scores into a source of local pride.

But the system has also exposed deep divides. In lower-income neighborhoods, residents say the cheapest options often carry the highest EI scores — ultra-processed foods shipped from far away or produced with heavy irrigation in water-stressed regions.

“I want to do the right thing. But when the low-EI rice costs twice as much, what choice do I really have?”

Jamal Washington · Detroit · climate justice volunteer

Advocates from the People’s EI Accountability Coalition argue that the index, while useful, still places too much burden on individual consumers rather than forcing upstream changes in agriculture and manufacturing. “We didn’t ask for a scorecard,” one organizer said at a recent protest outside a supermarket chain headquarters. “We asked for systems that make the sustainable choice the affordable one.”

Place & power June 4, 2040

Once Dismissed as “Green Theater,” Provincial EI Rankings Are Now Reshaping Where People Choose to Live and Work

↑ Nova Scotia offshore wind
↑ Minnesota Iron Range circular mfg
↓ Legacy steel regions tension

When the 2040 EI Progress & Decoupling Index rankings were released last month, few expected the top spots in North America to go to places like Nova Scotia and Minnesota’s Iron Range — regions long associated with heavy industry.

The index measures not just absolute emissions reductions but how cleanly economic activity has decoupled from environmental harm at the provincial and state level. Policymakers credit long-term investments in offshore wind, mine-site remediation, and circular manufacturing clusters.

Rankings now carry real weight. Large employers cite strong provincial EI scores when opening facilities or relocating workers. Real estate agents advertise “Low-EI Living.” Remote workers deliberately move to high-performing places for lifestyle and values alignment.

Not everyone is celebrating. In places that have slipped, residents describe frustration and political tension. “We’re being punished for industries that kept the lights on for the whole country for fifty years,” said a steelworker in Pennsylvania. Environmental groups counter that the index rewards genuine transition, not nostalgia.

The Global EI Justice Network has pushed for a “historical responsibility” adjustment — credit for cleaning up pollution legacies, not only rewarding cleaner baselines. The proposal has traction in several legislatures but remains controversial.

Global movements September 18, 2040

“This Index Was Supposed to Save Us. Now It’s Being Used Against Us.” Social Movements Demand a Justice-Centered EI Overhaul

On a humid afternoon in Manila, hundreds of activists gathered outside the regional offices of an international development bank. Fifteen years after widespread adoption of corporate, portfolio, and national Environmental Impact indices, a growing global movement argues the tools have been captured by powerful interests and are deepening inequality rather than solving the climate crisis.

Critics say portfolio EI metrics accelerated capital flight from the Global South; national rankings often ignore emissions embedded in exports serving rich-nation consumers; and companies celebrate intensity gains while workers in carbon-intensive regions report job losses without support.

“We were told these numbers would create accountability. Instead, they’re being used to justify trade barriers and deny financing to countries still building basic infrastructure.”

Aisha Rahman · Bangladesh · climate justice organizer

Origin story October 22, 2040 Looking back to 2026

How Three Founders With Very Different Skills Built a Tool That Made Environmental Tracking Routine for Startups

In early 2026, when most climate tools still required spreadsheets, consultants, or engineering teams, three people set out to change that for startups. Rae brought deep technical expertise in data infrastructure and emissions modeling. Gabriel focused on strategy — how the product could fit into funding, regulation, and day-to-day founder decisions. Tej handled design and prototyping, determined to make complex environmental data feel as intuitive as a modern financial dashboard.

The result was Climatico — a platform that let early-stage companies calculate, visualize, and track their Environmental Impact intensity (emissions per dollar of revenue) without a sustainability hire. Fourteen years later, the three founders have followed different paths, yet each continues to shape how environmental performance is measured and acted upon.

Early adopters say tracking EI intensity from the first funding round changed product design, supplier choices, and investor conversations. Some accelerators and funds began requiring such tools — influencing how portfolio-level EI metrics were calculated downstream. Critics warn easy dashboards can mask structural emissions outside the startup economy and create an illusion of precision. Even so, in many founder communities today, EI intensity sits alongside revenue and growth as a basic operating metric.
