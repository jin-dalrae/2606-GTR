When Environmental Impact intensity left the annual report — and rewrote how people shop, choose where to live, and fight for climate justice.

01 · Daily life March 12, 2040

In 2040, Your Grocery Receipt Now Shows an “EI Score.” For Some Families, It’s a Source of Pride — and Guilt.

Self-checkout · Phoenix
Oat milkEI 3
Rice · 2lbEI 18
Local produceEI 4
EI Intensity42
Maria 42 ↓ from 51

When Maria Lopez, a 38-year-old nurse in Phoenix, scans her groceries at the self-checkout, a small number appears at the bottom of her digital receipt: EI Intensity: 42. It is lower than last month’s 51. She smiles.

Across the United States and dozens of other countries, major retailers now display a standardized Environmental Impact Intensity score — a simplified version of the corporate EI/Revenue metrics companies have been required to publish since the mid-2030s. The number reflects the estimated footprint (carbon, water stress, and land use) embedded in each product, normalized for price.

For many households, the score has become a quiet daily ritual. Parents compare cereal brands. College students track a monthly “EI budget” the way earlier generations watched calories. Community groups in several states have created neighborhood leaderboards, turning lower scores into a source of local pride.

But the system has also exposed deep divides. In lower-income neighborhoods, residents say the cheapest options often carry the highest EI scores — ultra-processed foods shipped from far away or produced with heavy irrigation in water-stressed regions.

“I want to do the right thing. But when the low-EI rice costs twice as much, what choice do I really have?”

Jamal Washington · Detroit · climate justice volunteer

Advocates from the People’s EI Accountability Coalition argue that the index, while useful, still places too much burden on individual consumers rather than forcing upstream changes in agriculture and manufacturing. “We didn’t ask for a scorecard,” one organizer said at a protest outside a supermarket chain headquarters. “We asked for systems that make the sustainable choice the affordable one.”

02 · Place & power June 4, 2040

Once Dismissed as “Green Theater,” Provincial EI Rankings Are Now Reshaping Where People Choose to Live and Work

↑ Nova Scotia offshore wind
↑ Minnesota Iron Range circular mfg
↓ Legacy steel regions tension

When the 2040 EI Progress & Decoupling Index rankings were released last month, few expected the top spots in North America to go to places like Nova Scotia and Minnesota’s Iron Range — regions long associated with heavy industry.

Yet both have climbed dramatically. The index measures not just absolute emissions reductions but how cleanly economic activity has decoupled from environmental harm at the provincial and state level. Policymakers credit long-term investments in offshore wind, mine-site remediation, and circular manufacturing clusters.

The rankings now carry real weight. Several large employers have cited strong provincial EI scores when deciding where to open facilities or relocate workers. Real estate agents in high-ranking regions advertise “Low-EI Living.” Some remote workers deliberately move to places that perform well on the index, seeking both lifestyle and alignment with their values.

Not everyone is celebrating. In provinces and states that have slipped, residents describe frustration and political tension. “We’re being punished for industries that kept the lights on for the whole country for fifty years,” said a steelworker in a Pennsylvania town hall. Environmental groups counter that the index rewards genuine transition, not nostalgia.

The Global EI Justice Network wants a “historical responsibility” adjustment — credit for cleaning up pollution legacies, not only for cleaner baselines.

Proposal · several national legislatures · still controversial
03 · Global movements September 18, 2040

“This Index Was Supposed to Save Us. Now It’s Being Used Against Us.” Social Movements Demand a Justice-Centered EI Overhaul

EI Without Justice Is Just Green Colonialism
Our Emissions, Their Rankings
Historical responsibility now
Transition with workers

On a humid afternoon in Manila, hundreds of activists gathered outside the regional offices of an international development bank. Fifteen years after widespread adoption of corporate, portfolio, and national Environmental Impact indices, a growing global movement argues the tools have been captured by powerful interests and are deepening inequality rather than solving the climate crisis.

Critics point to several problems. Portfolio EI metrics, they say, have accelerated capital flight from the Global South as investors chase lower-intensity assets in already wealthy countries. National and provincial rankings often fail to account for emissions embedded in exported goods that ultimately serve consumers in rich nations. And while companies celebrate year-over-year intensity gains, many workers in carbon-intensive industries — from coal regions in India to oil towns in Nigeria and the U.S. Gulf Coast — report job losses without adequate support.

“We were told these numbers would create accountability. Instead, they’re being used to justify trade barriers and deny financing to countries still building basic infrastructure.”

Aisha Rahman · Bangladesh · climate justice organizer

In response, a coalition of more than 200 organizations has launched the People’s EI Charter. Some governments are experimenting with revised indices that blend environmental data with equity metrics. Whether those experiments scale — or the current generation of EI indices remains dominant — has become one of the defining political battles of the 2040s.

People’s EI Charter · 200+ organizations
  • Historical emissions & consumption-based accounting in national indices
  • Stronger protections for workers and communities in transition
  • Indigenous and frontline groups help define how “impact” is measured
