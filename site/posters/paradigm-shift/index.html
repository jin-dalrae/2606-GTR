Paradigm shift

The scoreboard changes: EI intensity runs daily life, place, and politics

Ten years after EI became capital language, it rewrites the social scoreboard — how people shop, where they live and work, and who gets to define “impact.”

What is EI?

Environmental Impact (EI) is the measured harm a product, company, fund, or place causes — usually greenhouse gases (tCO₂e), and often water stress and land use as well. Built from activity data × emission factors (GHG Protocol for companies).

Scope 1 direct Scope 2 power Scope 3 value chain
The number on every receipt & ranking

What is EI intensity?

Absolute tonnes alone punish scale. Intensity divides total impact by value created — so a large firm and a small one (or two grocery baskets) can be compared fairly.

EI intensity = Total EI (e.g. tCO₂e) ÷ value unit (revenue, $ spent, GDP…) Lower intensity = more economic (or consumer) value per unit of environmental harm.
Why it becomes the scoreboard

From capital language → civic language

Once intensity is public and standardized, it leaves investor decks and shows up on receipts, provincial ranks, and political fights over who the metric serves — the three stories below.

When Maria Lopez, a 38-year-old nurse in Phoenix, scans her groceries, a small number appears: EI intensity: 42 — down from 51 last month. She smiles.

Across the U.S. and dozens of countries, major retailers display a standardized Environmental Impact intensity score — a consumer version of the corporate EI/Revenue metrics companies have published since the mid-2030s. Carbon, water stress, and land use, normalized for price.

Parents compare cereal brands. College students track a monthly “EI budget” the way earlier generations watched calories. Community groups run neighborhood leaderboards. Pride for some; pressure for others when the cheapest food scores worst — ultra-processed goods shipped far or grown with heavy irrigation in water-stressed regions.

“I want to do the right thing. But when the low-EI rice costs twice as much, what choice do I really have?”

Jamal Washington · Detroit · climate justice volunteer

The People’s EI Accountability Coalition says the index still loads too much onto shoppers. They didn’t ask for a scorecard — they asked for systems that make the sustainable choice the affordable one.

02 · Place & power June 4, 2040

Once dismissed as “green theater,” provincial EI rankings now reshape where people live and work.

↑ Nova Scotia offshore wind
↑ Minnesota Iron Range circular mfg
↓ Legacy steel regions tension

When the 2040 EI Progress & Decoupling Index landed, few expected North American leaders to include Nova Scotia and Minnesota’s Iron Range — regions long tied to heavy industry.

The index measures not only absolute emissions cuts but how cleanly economic activity has decoupled from environmental harm. Policymakers credit offshore wind, mine-site remediation, and circular manufacturing clusters.

Rankings now carry weight. Large employers cite strong provincial EI scores when opening facilities or relocating workers. Real estate agents advertise “Low-EI Living.” Remote workers move for lifestyle and values alignment. Places that slip describe frustration — and political tension.

“We’re being punished for industries that kept the lights on for fifty years.” Environmental groups counter: the index rewards transition, not nostalgia.

Steelworker · Pennsylvania town hall

The Global EI Justice Network wants a “historical responsibility” adjustment — credit for cleaning up pollution legacies, not only for cleaner baselines. Traction in several legislatures; still contested.

03 · Global movements September 18, 2040

“This index was supposed to save us. Now it’s being used against us.”

Transition with workers

On a humid afternoon in Manila, hundreds of activists surround a development bank. Fifteen years into corporate, portfolio, and national Environmental Impact indices, a growing movement says the tools were captured — deepening inequality instead of solving the climate crisis.

Portfolio EI metrics accelerate capital flight from the Global South as investors chase lower-intensity assets in already wealthy countries. National ranks often ignore emissions embedded in exports that serve rich-nation consumers. Companies celebrate intensity gains while workers in coal, oil, and heavy industry report job losses without support.

“We were told these numbers would create accountability. Instead, they’re used to justify trade barriers and deny financing to countries still building basic infrastructure.”

Aisha Rahman · Bangladesh · climate justice organizer

A coalition of more than 200 organizations has launched the People’s EI Charter. Some governments experiment with indices that blend environmental data and equity metrics. Whether those scale — or current EI indices stay dominant — is one of the defining political fights of the 2040s.

People’s EI Charter · 200+ organizations
  • Historical emissions & consumption-based accounting in national indices
  • Stronger protections for workers and communities in transition
  • Indigenous and frontline groups help define how “impact” is measured
