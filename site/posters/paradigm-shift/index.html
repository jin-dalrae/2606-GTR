Paradigm shift

The scoreboard changes: EI intensity runs daily life, place, and politics

Ten years after EI became capital language, it rewrites how people shop, where they live and work, and who gets to define “impact.”

Horizon year
2040

Quick primer

EI → EI intensity → civic scoreboard

EI is total environmental load (usually tCO₂e, Scope 1–2–3). Intensity divides that total by value — so scale is fair.

EI intensity = Total EI ÷ value (revenue, $ spent, GDP…) Lower = more value per unit of environmental harm.

Daily life March 12, 2040

Your grocery receipt shows an “EI Score.” Pride for some — pressure for others.

At checkout, Maria Lopez sees EI intensity: 42 — down from 51. Retailers show a consumer version of the corporate intensity metrics that went public in the mid-2030s.

Students track an “EI budget.” Neighborhoods run leaderboards. The cheapest cart often scores worst — and the fight becomes whether the sustainable choice is the affordable one.

“I want to do the right thing. But when the low-EI rice costs twice as much, what choice do I really have?”

Jamal Washington · Detroit
Place & power June 4, 2040

Provincial EI rankings reshape where people live and work.

The EI Progress & Decoupling Index lifts places like Nova Scotia and Minnesota’s Iron Range — not only for cutting tonnes, but for cleanly decoupling growth from harm.

Employers cite ranks when relocating. Agents sell “Low-EI Living.” Regions that slip feel punished for industries that once “kept the lights on.”

↑ Nova Scotia · wind ↑ Iron Range · circular ↓ Legacy steel

“We’re being punished for industries that kept the lights on for fifty years.” Counters: the index rewards transition, not nostalgia.

Steelworker · Pennsylvania town hall
Global movements September 18, 2040

“This index was supposed to save us. Now it’s being used against us.”

In Manila, activists say portfolio and national EI tools were captured — capital flight, ignored export emissions, intensity “wins” that hide workers left behind.

A People’s EI Charter (200+ orgs) demands historical responsibility, consumption-based accounting, and frontline voices in how “impact” is defined.

“We were told these numbers would create accountability. Instead, they’re used to deny financing to countries still building basic infrastructure.”

Aisha Rahman · Bangladesh
