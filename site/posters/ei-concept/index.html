These ideas help measure, normalize, and compare environmental performance (usually greenhouse gases — also water, waste, biodiversity) across scales. Normalization by revenue, capital, GDP, or per capita enables apples-to-apples comparisons. Progress indices track improvement toward goals like net-zero or the UN SDGs.

01 — Company-wise EI

Total footprint, then intensity.

Quantify a company’s total environmental footprint, then divide by revenue (or employees, units, capital) to show how much environmental harm per dollar of economic value created. Primary method: GHG Protocol — the global standard for corporate emissions.

Scope 1 · Direct

Owned & controlled

Emissions from sources the company owns or controls: on-site fuel, fleet vehicles, process emissions.

Closest to the chimney — usually easiest to meter.

Scope 2 · Energy

Purchased power

Indirect emissions from bought electricity, steam, heating, and cooling.

Location and supplier mix change the number fast.

Scope 3 · Value chain

Everything else

Upstream (goods, transport, capital) and downstream (product use, end-of-life, investments). 15 categories.

Often the largest share — and the hardest to measure.

How EI is built

Activity data (fuel, kWh, supplier surveys) × emission factors (IPCC, EPA, national databases).

Credibility

Third-party verification and frameworks: CDP, GRI, ISSB. Broader EI can monetize impacts or use multi-impact LCA.

Why normalize

Absolute tonnes alone punish scale. Intensity shows efficiency — fair peer and sector benchmarks.

01b — EI / Revenue intensity

The comparison that levels size.

Key normalization formula
$$\text{EI Intensity (e.g. Carbon Intensity)} = \frac{\text{Total EI (tCO}_2\text{e or other units)}}{\text{Revenue (e.g. USD millions)}}$$

Lower intensity = better carbon productivity — more economic value per unit of environmental impact. Same idea extends to EI per employee, per unit shipped, or per $ invested capital.

Company A
Emissions500,000 tCO₂e
Revenue$10 billion
50 tCO₂e per $M revenue
Lower intensity · more efficient
Company B
Emissions100,000 tCO₂e
Revenue$1 billion
100 tCO₂e per $M revenue
Higher intensity · less efficient

Company A has higher absolute emissions — but lower intensity. On a per-dollar basis, A creates more value with less environmental harm.

Where intensity is used

  • Internal targets (e.g. Science Based Targets initiative — SBTi)
  • ESG reporting and investor questionnaires
  • Peer and sector benchmarking
  • Spotting efficiency gains or “hidden liabilities”

Challenges to watch

  • Scope 3 gaps and heavy estimation
  • Revenue currency and inflation adjustments
  • Sector differences (oil & gas vs software)
  • Inconsistent organizational boundaries

02 — Investor portfolio EI

Financed emissions & portfolio comparison.

Aggregate the environmental impacts “financed” by an investor’s loans, equity, bonds, or other holdings — the investor’s Scope 3 Category 15 (financed emissions). Enables comparison of portfolios on climate risk, Paris alignment, or transition readiness.

Standard PCAF — Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials · Global GHG Accounting & Reporting Standard (banks, asset managers, insurers) carbonaccountingfinancials.com
Core formula · most asset classes
$$\text{Financed Emissions (tCO}_2\text{e)} = \sum (\text{Attribution Factor}_i \times \text{Emissions of company/project } i)$$

Attribution is the investor’s proportional share of the company or project — not 100% of investee emissions.

Listed equity & corporate bonds

Outstanding investment ÷ EVIC (Enterprise Value Including Cash).

Business loans / unlisted equity

Outstanding amount ÷ (total equity + debt) of the company.

Other asset classes

Project finance, commercial real estate, mortgages, etc. — each has tailored PCAF rules.

PCAF coverage

Parts A / B / C: financed emissions, facilitated emissions, insurance-associated emissions.

Portfolio metrics

  • Absolute financed emissions — total tCO₂e or MtCO₂e
  • Intensity / footprint — financed emissions ÷ portfolio value (e.g. tCO₂e per $M invested)
  • WACI — weighted average carbon intensity: portfolio-weight × company revenue intensity, summed (transition-risk lens)

Comparison uses

  • Vs. benchmarks (MSCI World, S&P 500, sector peers)
  • Vs. net-zero / 1.5°C–2°C pathways (e.g. PACTA)
  • Portfolio construction: tilt to lower-intensity or transitioning firms
  • Regulatory disclosure (ISSB, EU CSRD, emerging SEC rules)
  • “Avoided emissions” / positive-impact tracking for green holdings

Challenges

  • Data gaps (private cos., Scope 3)
  • Attribution choice can swing results
  • Double-counting across investors
  • Historical vs. forward-looking focus
03 — Nation / province EI + progress index

Compare places. Track improvement over years.

Compare environmental performance across countries or sub-national regions using absolute totals + normalized metrics, then build time-series indices for improvement, decoupling from growth, or policy effectiveness.

Data & calculation

  • National: UNFCCC GHG inventories (territorial). Sources: EDGAR, Global Carbon Project, Climate Watch (WRI), national stats. Also consumption-based accounting (trade-embodied).
  • Province / state: US EPA State Inventory Tool; detailed data in CA, EU regions, Chinese provincial inventories. Watch electricity trade allocation and LULUCF.

Common comparison metrics

  • Absolute emissions (MtCO₂e)
  • Per capita (tCO₂e / person)
  • Intensity per GDP (tCO₂e / $GDP, PPP or nominal)
  • Sectoral breakdowns + air quality, water, biodiversity, waste
Simple emission reduction index

Base year = 100

$$\text{Emission Index}_y = \left( \frac{\text{Emissions}_y}{\text{Emissions}_{\text{base}}} \right) \times 100$$

70 in 2025 vs. 1990 base = 30% reduction — easy for public dashboards.

Decoupling index

Emissions vs. economic growth

$$\text{Decoupling Index} = \frac{\%\Delta\text{ Emissions}}{\%\Delta\text{ GDP}}$$

Values < 1 (or negative) = relative or absolute decoupling — the goal for sustainable growth.

Composite indices

EPI-style rankings

Yale/Columbia Environmental Performance Index ranks ~180 countries on 50+ indicators (climate, health, ecosystems). Peer groups by region or income level; sub-national adaptations exist.

Target / trajectory index

On-track vs. overshoot

Compare actual pathway to required linear or science-based reduction for net-zero by 2050. Score acceleration / deceleration year-over-year. Ties to NDC tracking under Paris.

Applications

  • Policy evaluation (carbon tax, renewable subsidies)
  • Investment risk / green-growth opportunity maps
  • Corporate supply-chain and site decisions
  • Public dashboards & Paris NDC progress

Challenges

  • Data consistency across regions
  • Transboundary impacts (imported power / goods)
  • Weighting choices in composite indices
  • Granularity gaps at province level
