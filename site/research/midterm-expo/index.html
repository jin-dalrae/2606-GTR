Overview

Date July 13, 2026 Materials Tabloid poster series · 3-monitor demo Product Climatico (founder EI dashboard + assessment)

Team GTR presented the Climatico concept: early-stage startups get a lightweight assessment and dashboard so environmental impact (EI) is estimated from day one — past/present snapshot plus growth-coupled future — with accelerator and investor views for cohorts and portfolios.

The prototype landed as usable and exciting. The sharpest critique was not “why climate,” but the gap between a score and a founder’s Monday morning: actionable steps, resource links, cost correlation, progressive disclosure, and clear naming.

Four pillars from the floor

Denise

Monday-morning “how”

Metrics without a playbook fail. Climatico should be a repository: steps, external links, assignable tasks, and progress when new data is logged.

Vic

Business value

Climate “why” is clear; CEO “why” is not. Pair every carbon insight with cost, risk, or revenue language.

Leslie · Katya

Density & IA

Progressive disclosure: primary metrics first, detail on demand. Icons over long copy. Don’t scare non-specialists on first glance.

Neha · Marc

Naming & media

Spell out Environmental Impact on first use. Soften academic poster grids toward publication design and imagery.

Cross-cutting theme: visitors believed the early-intervention thesis (startups scale fast; ESG obligation arrives late). They pushed the product to be operational — believable inputs, clear “how,” and a reason to open the dashboard again next week.

Design implications (for the next sprint)

  1. Actionable insight cards — each action has: what · why (EI + $) · step-by-step how · external resources · “assign to teammate” / re-measure prompt.
  2. Methodology transparency — show what enters the model (travel, cloud, headcount, manufacturing, etc.), who measures (Climatico vs Workday/Salesforce vs defaults), and confidence.
  3. Progressive disclosure — dashboard overview first; drill-downs second. Reduce first-screen density on investor/pitch views.
  4. Always expand EI — “Environmental Impact (EI)” and “EI intensity (impact ÷ revenue)” on first use in UI, posters, and pitch.
  5. Icons + less body copy — especially onboarding and assessment report; keep Katya’s “intentional hierarchy,” fix the text walls.
  6. Poster / editorial pass — less academic grid; more imagery, callouts, publication pacing (Marc).
  7. Business hook in the story — acquisition and onboarding copy answer Vic’s ROI test before the climate thesis.
  8. Habit loop — weekly reminder to log actions + new data so founders see bad → neutral → good (Denise’s progress narrative).
  9. Infer, don’t confirm (Elena) — when URL / pitch / model already implies what the company does, pre-fill and move on; skip a heavy “confirm what your company does” gate.

IA & density

Leslie and peers on first-look cognitive load.

Presenter

This is the onboarding stage — mobile is the easy entry. Then the founder dashboard: how the business is running, environmental impact cascaded by stage, and the behavior change we’re aiming for. Accelerator cohorts, program directors, and later investors share a system view of the metrics.

Leslie

How is data gathered into the dashboard? How is it reported?

Presenter

First we hook them: website or pitch-deck data — subjective, but enough to estimate impact. After account creation, connect Salesforce / ops tools for better data.

Leslie

My only real feedback is it feels like a lot of data all at once. Play with the information architecture. This is fine… this is great… this scares me. I’m not your target audience — I don’t usually review pitch decks — but for me that’s so much information.

Takeaway: density is the first-frame risk for non-climate reviewers and possibly for real VCs skimming. Progressive disclosure is a product requirement, not polish.

How data is gathered (trust)

Visitors wanted the chain of custody: assessment → model → integrations → what is actually measured.

Presenter

Onboarding is simplified estimation (headcount, funding stage, model). Later: connect Workday / Salesforce / accounting — travel, remote vs office, spend categories — for precision. Dashboard updates; insights stay current.

Visitor

It would help to see what is calculated — travel, manufacturing, cloud — so the number is believable. Are you measuring? Is Workday? Someone else? Also: am I as an individual responsible for company impact vs my home life?

Presenter

More than the sum of individuals, but headcount matters. We should expose calculation inputs and boundaries more clearly in the UI.

Takeaway: show a “what’s in this number” slice early in concept storytelling (even if deep detail lives later in the tool). Boundary clarity reduces “is this me or my company?” confusion.

Monday-morning “how”

The strongest single critique of the day — after assessment + dashboard, founders still don’t know what to do.

Denise

I have my dashboard, I’m fired up. How do I actually lower things? How do I change what I need to change?

Presenter

Insights in the dashboard, part by part — we’re still prototyping that section.

Denise

I see “make lower-impact decisions during product development and operations.” How? Don’t just tell me the action. Step by step. What I need when I talk to my team on Monday.

Presenter

Example: sourcing from a Chinese factory vs a US factory — slightly more expensive, maybe half the EI.

Denise

That still doesn’t say how. You don’t have to solve the whole supply chain. Be a repository: links, articles, videos, podcasts. I assign Gabriel research, Tej an action. Next week I log new data; bad → neutral → good. Remind me: “Did you do this? Put your new numbers in.” Tell me how — not only that I should.

Product shape: insight = action card + how-to path + external knowledge + team assignment + re-measure loop. “Repository of how” is the differentiator vs a static score.

Business bottom line

Climate motivation is insufficient for many operators.

Harshit

What’s the motivation for startups to pay attention to this?

Presenter

Big companies must report ESG / emissions. Startups don’t — so on the way up they can lock in high-emitting infrastructure (e.g. opaque data-center use). Intervene early. Low-cost estimation vs expensive enterprise measurement later.

Vic

I get the climate why. Why should I care as a business owner? Cost? Revenue? Cheaper to operate? How do you get people to sign up?

Visitor

Also in onboarding: keep reminding people of the larger purpose — it’s easy to start conscious and then drop it when it’s hard.

Takeaway: dual hook — (1) lock-in / future reporting / capital, (2) operational $ savings. Purpose microcopy in intake so motivation doesn’t only live on the poster.

EI naming & scope

Visitor

What is tCO₂e?

Presenter

Tonnes of CO₂ equivalent. EI is environmental impact; EI intensity is EI divided by revenue — absolute EI can look “good” just because the company is small.

Neha

What does EI mean? Outside climate, EI is emotional intelligence. Specify that. Posters also feel like university research boards.

Presenter

Today we focus on CO₂; longer term water, waste, sector-specific factors. Frameworks are existing; presentation needs to be more approachable.

Takeaway: expand EI on first use everywhere; keep intensity next to absolute; plan multi-impact but don’t pretend full multi-factor today.

Visual & editorial feedback

Katya is the careful investigator: she oriented herself on the materials first, then gave a design-literate compliment with one constructive edge.

Katya

I think it’s very intuitive, especially the onboarding — this is the onboard, right? The onboarding is very intuitive and exciting. My only feedback: I wish you could add some icons so it’s not just text. But the layout makes a lot of sense; you prioritized the most important information, so there isn’t cognitive overload. The design feels very intentional. Well done.

Team

You sound like a professional UX designer — thank you.

Marc

At first glance it reads very academic. Make it more approachable — digital publication design, not paragraphs on paragraphs. NYT-style editorial. Add imagery; environment is visual.

Visitor

On the first assessment report: make “how did you calculate this?” and improvement paths larger — founders care about where costs come from and what to fix.

Katya’s posture: inspect → affirm what’s working (hierarchy, intent, onboarding clarity) → one precise ask (semantic icons). Different from Leslie’s density scare or Marc’s academic critique — she validated the design system while sharpening micro-visuals.

Takeaway overall: two surfaces, two jobs — tool (icons, hierarchy, how-to) and story artifacts (posters/newsletter as publication, not academic wall).

Elena · lighter intake

Visitor with an Italian accent — captured in the team’s end-of-floor debrief.

Team

Elena was suggesting: instead of asking founders to confirm what the company does, we should take what the company does — so that step isn’t a big gate.

Elena

Prefer the product to infer company activity from what it already has (URL, pitch, model signals) rather than making the founder re-confirm or re-describe the business at onboarding.

Takeaway: starting-point friction was a team theme the same day (“prototype is good; getting into it is the hard part”). Elena’s fix is concrete: auto-take company profile → fewer confirm steps → faster first value.
