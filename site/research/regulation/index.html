How business is regulated

Federal climate disclosure may swing, but state rules, EPA baselines, and enterprise buyer pressure still define what startups must eventually produce.

On this page

  1. Mandatory for all
  2. Large-entity triggers
  3. Voluntary metrics

At the U.S. federal level, the SEC climate rule has been politically contested. That does not mean “nothing applies.” Physical thresholds, state law, and buyer requirements remain firmly in force.

1. Mandatory for all companies (threshold-based)

Regardless of revenue, if operations cross environmental thresholds or handle regulated materials, you must monitor and report to EPA or state equivalents.

Hazardous wasteUnder RCRA, generators above ~220 lbs/month track generation, storage, and cradle-to-grave manifests.
WastewaterProcess discharges into waters or sewers may require NPDES-style monitoring (COD, pH, metals).
Criteria air pollutantsBoilers and manufacturing lines above district thresholds: NOₓ, SOₓ, VOCs, particulate matter.

2. Big-company mandatory ranges

There is no single definition of “big company.” Different laws use different revenue or emissions triggers.

Regulation Trigger What you monitor
EPA GHGRP ≥ 25,000 tCO₂e/yr at a facility Scope 1 (direct) only
California SB 253 > $1B global revenue, doing business in CA Scope 1–2 first; Scope 3 follows
California SB 261 > $500M global revenue, CA business Climate-related financial risk disclosures
EPA TRI ≥ 10 FTE + industry + chemical thresholds Toxic releases & management quantities
